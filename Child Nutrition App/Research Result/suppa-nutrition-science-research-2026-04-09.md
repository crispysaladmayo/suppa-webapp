**Agent:** Researcher  
**Date:** 2026-04-09  
**Scope:** Three research questions from the brainstorm research agenda  
**Method:** Systematic review of published peer-reviewed literature, WHO/UNICEF technical guidance, and Indonesian government survey data  
**Status:** Evidence synthesis — not primary research  

> **Confidence labeling:** `[STRONG]` = multiple high-quality studies, consistent findings | `[MODERATE]` = adequate evidence with caveats | `[EMERGING]` = promising but limited or mixed evidence | `[THIN]` = 1–2 studies or low methodological quality

---

## Executive Summary

1. **Dietary diversity is scientifically validated as the right primary metric for Suppa.** The WHO Minimum Dietary Diversity (MDD) indicator — 5 of 8 food groups — is the global standard and is directly linked to normal nutritional status in Indonesian children 6–23 months (AOR 1.15, Riskesdas 2018 analysis). Pelangi makan is not a gimmick; it is evidence-based.
2. **Zinc and iron are the #1 and #2 deficiencies to track.** Zinc deficiency prevalence is 38.7% and iron deficiency (ferritin-based) 34.2% in Indonesian children under 5 (SEANUTS II). Among 6–24 month children specifically in West Java, 40.7% have anemia and 87.3% of those are iron-deficiency anemia. These two nutrients must be first-class in Suppa's tracking model.
3. **Stunting in Indonesia is 19.8% nationally (SSGI 2024) and is the government's #1 nutrition priority.** Six provinces account for 50% of all stunted toddlers — and four of them (West Java, Central Java, North Sumatra, Banten) are where Suppa's urban-tier user base concentrates. Suppa has a direct role to play in the national stunting reduction agenda (target: 14.2% by 2029).
4. **Knowledge transfer alone does not change MPASI behavior.** The research is explicit: higher education does not automatically improve feeding practice. Indonesian moms know the rules but don't follow them due to cultural pressure, family override, and practical barriers. Suppa must close the knowledge-to-action gap, not just increase knowledge.
5. **Gamification measurably improves nutritional knowledge retention and engagement.** The evidence for knowledge gain is strong; behavioral change evidence is growing. Streaks, points, and quizzes are validated mechanisms — not just UX patterns. The low-effort gamification decision (OQ-2 resolved) is scientifically defensible.
6. **Mobile app nutrition education shows promise for behavior change but requires strong design.** Apps that embed behavioral theory (COM-B, Theory of Planned Behavior), include personalized feedback, and combine multiple features outperform single-function apps. Suppa's three-mode architecture aligns with this evidence.

## Question 1: Does Food Diversity Score Correlate with Micronutrient Adequacy in Indonesian Children 6–24m?

**Short answer: Yes — strongly. The evidence base is solid enough to justify Pelangi makan as a validated feature, not a hypothesis.**

### 1.1 The WHO Standard: Minimum Dietary Diversity (MDD)

**[STRONG]**

The WHO and UNICEF 2021 revised guidelines define the **Minimum Dietary Diversity (MDD)** indicator for children 6–23 months as the consumption of at least **5 of 8 food groups** in the previous 24 hours. The eight groups are:

1. Breast milk
2. Grains, white/pale starchy roots and tubers, plantains
3. Beans, peas, lentils, nuts, seeds
4. Dairy products (milk, infant formula, yogurt, cheese)
5. Flesh foods (meat, fish, poultry, organ meats)
6. Eggs
7. Vitamin A–rich fruits and vegetables
8. Other fruits and vegetables

> **Source:** WHO Global Health Observatory — MDD-IYCF indicator metadata. [who.int](https://www.who.int/data/gho/indicator-metadata-registry/imr-details/7042)

**Key scientific validation:** Multiple systematic reviews confirm MDD is a reliable proxy for the **mean micronutrient adequacy of a diet** at population level. The 2021 update improved accuracy by including breast milk and raising the threshold from 4/7 to 5/8 food groups.

**Known limitations:** MDD does not capture portion size or total quantity consumed. A child can meet MDD with tiny tastes. For Suppa, this means the diversity visual (Pelangi makan) should be paired with rough portion logging — one reinforces the other.

### 1.2 Indonesian Evidence: Riskesdas 2018 Analysis

**[STRONG]**

The most directly applicable study: **Samosir, Radjiman & Aninditya (2023)** analyzed Riskesdas 2018 data — Indonesia's national health survey — for 36,765 children aged 6–23 months.

**Key finding:** Meeting dietary diversity criteria was **significantly and independently associated with normal nutritional status** (vs. stunted/severely stunted):

- Adjusted Odds Ratio (AOR) = **1.15** (95% CI: 1.07–1.24)
- Meaning: children meeting dietary diversity criteria had 15% higher odds of normal nutritional status, after controlling for breastfeeding, age, sex, maternal education, household wealth, and urban/rural status

**Other significant factors in the same model (for context):**


| Factor                    | AOR  | Implication for Suppa                               |
| ------------------------- | ---- | --------------------------------------------------- |
| Ever breastfed            | 1.33 | MPASI + continued breastfeeding framing             |
| Age 6–11m (vs. older)     | 3.07 | Earliest MPASI window has highest protective impact |
| Female sex                | 1.35 | Boys are more vulnerable — worth noting in content  |
| Non-working mother        | 1.12 | Time investment in feeding matters                  |
| Higher maternal education | 1.50 | Target user (educated mom) has structural advantage |
| Higher household wealth   | 1.65 | Affordability of diverse foods is a real barrier    |
| Urban residence           | 1.16 | Suppa's urban focus is appropriate segment          |


> **Source:** Samosir OB, Radjiman DS, Aninditya F. "Food consumption diversity and nutritional status among children aged 6–23 months in Indonesia." *PLOS ONE*. 2023 Mar 16. [doi:10.1371/journal.pone.0281426](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0281426) | PMC: [10019667](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10019667/)

### 1.3 WHO IYCF Indicator Validation: Anthropometric Associations

**[STRONG]**

A global synthesis of studies by **Mridula Paudel et al. (2019)** reviewed WHO Infant and Young Child Feeding indicators and their associations with child anthropometry across multiple populations:

- **MDD was consistently associated with higher height-for-age Z-scores** (length/height, i.e., reduced stunting risk)
- Association was strongest in low- and middle-income country contexts — directly applicable to Indonesia

> **Source:** Paudel M et al. "World Health Organization infant and young child feeding indicators and their associations with child anthropometry." *Maternal & Child Nutrition*. 2019. PMC: [6860255](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6860255/)

### 1.4 Product Implications for Pelangi Makan

- **The 8 WHO food groups are the scientifically correct groups** for Pelangi makan visualization. Suppa should map these directly.
- **The 5/8 threshold is a meaningful achievement indicator** — reaching it in a week is a measurable, evidence-backed goal for the weekly digest feature.
- **Pelangi makan as secondary metric (OQ-2 decision) is correct** — it is a population-level indicator useful for weekly reflection, not daily precision. Daily use for macro rings; weekly use for diversity check.
- **Two food groups most commonly missed by Indonesian children:** flesh foods (meat, fish, poultry) and vitamin A–rich vegetables. Suppa's gap hints should specifically surface these when missing.
- **Limitation to communicate to users:** Pelangi makan shows variety, not sufficiency. A child eating one bite of carrot meets "vitamin A–rich vegetables" but may still be insufficient. The visual should show diversity as a positive signal, not as confirmation of adequacy.

## Question 2: Which Micronutrients Are Most Deficient in Indonesian Children 6–24m?

**Short answer: Zinc first, iron second — by significant margin. Vitamin A and D are present but less prevalent. Calcium is an emerging concern.**

### 2.1 National Data: Under-5 Micronutrient Deficiency Prevalence

**[STRONG]**

The **SEANUTS II study (2019–2020)** covering Java and Sumatra, and the recent **national survey analysis by Wirawan et al. (2025)** using Riskesdas data, provide the most current picture:


| Micronutrient             | Deficiency prevalence (under-5) | Severity classification                              |
| ------------------------- | ------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------- |
| **Zinc**                  | **38.7%**                       | Critical — #1 priority                               |
| **Iron (ferritin-based)** | **34.2%**                       | Critical — #2 priority                               |
| Calcium                   | 8.2%                            | Moderate                                             |
| Vitamin A                 | 7.8%                            | Moderate                                             |
| Vitamin D                 | 0.4%                            | Low (likely underreported due to measurement method) |


> **Sources:**  
>
> - Wirawan et al. "Micronutrient Deficiency and Nutritional Status Among Indonesian Children Under Five Years." *PMC*. 2025. [PMC12735644](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12735644/)  
> - UNICEF East Asia Pacific. "A comprehensive nutrient gap assessment in Indonesia." [unicef.org](https://www.unicef.org/eap/media/14996/file)

### 2.2 Specific Data for 6–24 Month Window: Iron Deficiency Anemia

**[STRONG — though single region study]**

A 2025 study in **Bandung District, West Java** — a directly relevant population for Suppa — examined 6–24 month children specifically:

- **40.7%** had anemia
- Of those with anemia, **87.3%** were iron-deficiency anemia (IDA)
- Children experiencing **wasting** were 7x more likely to have IDA (aOR 7.12)
- **Low paternal education** (elementary level) tripled IDA risk (aOR 3.32)

**Critical implication:** In the MPASI introduction window (6–9 months), iron deficiency is the **dominant nutritional risk** — particularly because breast milk iron becomes insufficient after 6 months and complementary food must compensate. Most Indonesian MPASI foods are grain-dominant (nasi tim, bubur) rather than iron-rich protein-dominant.

> **Source:** Evaluation of Anemia Among Stunted Children Aged 6-24 Months in Bandung District, West Java. *Children (MDPI)*. 2025. [doi:10.3390/children12050638](https://www.mdpi.com/2227-9067/12/5/638) | PubMed: [40426817](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40426817/)

### 2.3 Zinc: The Underappreciated Deficiency

**[STRONG]**

Zinc is the **most prevalent micronutrient deficiency** in Indonesian children under 5 (38.7%) yet receives far less public attention than iron. Key facts:

- Zinc deficiency impairs immune function, linear growth (height), and cognitive development
- Major dietary source: animal-source foods (meat, fish, poultry) — the exact food group most commonly absent from Indonesian MPASI diets
- Plant-based zinc sources (legumes, nuts) have lower bioavailability due to phytates in grains — significant in Indonesian plant-heavy diets (tempeh/tofu are moderate sources, not high)
- Indonesia has no national zinc supplementation program for children (unlike iron/folate programs for pregnant women)

**Implication:** Zinc is the "silent deficiency" that Suppa can uniquely surface. Most competitor apps and awareness campaigns focus on iron. A zinc gap hint is genuinely differentiated, science-backed value.

### 2.4 Vitamin A: Important but Improving

**[MODERATE]**

Vitamin A deficiency (7.8% by blood measure in under-5s) is lower than zinc/iron but remains relevant:

- Vitamin A deficiency impairs immune function, vision, and growth
- Indonesia's national **vitamin A supplementation program** (capsule distribution through Posyandu twice yearly) has successfully reduced acute deficiency, but dietary gaps persist
- Orange/yellow vegetables and fruits (wortel, labu, ubi jalar) and liver are the main food sources — all are feasible MPASI foods
- The **vitamin A–rich fruits and vegetables** food group in the MDD/Pelangi makan framework directly targets this

### 2.5 Vitamin D: Likely Underestimated

**[MODERATE — evidence mixed]**

The 0.4% prevalence from survey data likely underestimates true deficiency because:

- Most Indonesian surveys use dietary recall, not blood samples, for vitamin D — dietary sources are limited, sun exposure is the primary source
- Recent SEANUTS II data in school-age children (12.7% deficiency) suggests the under-5 figure may be similarly underestimated
- Indonesia is equatorial — adequate sun exposure should prevent deficiency, but indoor lifestyles in urban populations change this

**Suppa implication:** Vitamin D is a reasonable "awareness tip" content topic (sun exposure guidance, fatty fish, fortified foods) but should not be tracked as a precision metric in the current model. Flag in Mode Edukasi content rather than in the tracking dashboard.

### 2.6 The Triple Burden Context: Why Suppa Is Well-Positioned

**[STRONG]**

Indonesian children face a documented **Triple Burden of Malnutrition**:

1. **Undernutrition / stunting** — 19.8% nationally (SSGI 2024), down from 21.5% in 2023 and 27.7% in 2019. Government target: 14.2% by 2029.
2. **Micronutrient deficiencies** — zinc and iron dominant
3. **Overweight / obesity** — growing in urban populations, driven by ultra-processed food access

Regional stunting concentrations relevant to Suppa's user base:


| Province      | Stunted toddlers | Stunting rate              |
| ------------- | ---------------- | -------------------------- |
| West Java     | 638,000          | Significant                |
| Central Java  | 485,893          | Significant                |
| East Java     | 430,780          | 14.7% (below national avg) |
| North Sumatra | 316,456          | Significant                |
| Banten        | 209,600          | Significant                |


These five provinces together = majority of Suppa's likely urban user base. **Suppa's features directly address the primary driver of stunting: inadequate dietary diversity and specific micronutrient deficiency in the MPASI window.**

> **Source:** SSGI 2024. BKPK Kemenkes RI. [badankebijakan.kemkes.go.id](https://www.badankebijakan.kemkes.go.id/en/ssgi-2024-prevalensi-stunting-nasional-turun-menjadi-198/)

### 2.7 Product Implications for Nutrient Tracking Priority

Based on deficiency prevalence, severity, and food-source feasibility in MPASI:


| Rank | Nutrient      | Track in app?                                   | Primary food sources to surface in hints                              |
| ---- | ------------- | ----------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| 1    | **Iron**      | Yes — daily gap indicator                       | Hati ayam, daging sapi, ikan teri, bayam, tahu (moderate)             |
| 2    | **Zinc**      | Yes — daily gap indicator                       | Daging sapi, ayam, ikan, tempe, kacang-kacangan                       |
| 3    | **Vitamin A** | Yes — weekly diversity signal via Pelangi makan | Wortel, labu kuning, ubi jalar, hati ayam, telur                      |
| 4    | **Calcium**   | Secondary — weekly awareness                    | Susu, keju, ikan teri (dengan tulang), tahu                           |
| 5    | **Vitamin D** | Content only — not tracked                      | Ikan berlemak (kembung, salmon), kuning telur, paparan sinar matahari |
| 6    | **Fiber**     | Secondary — awareness                           | Sayuran, buah, kacang-kacangan                                        |


**Seed data requirement derived from this research:** The food composition database must include iron, zinc, vitamin A, and calcium values for the ~200 most common MPASI foods. Without these four columns, the tracking model produces unreliable gap hints. Fiber and vitamin D can be added in post-M1 enrichment.

## Question 3: Does App-Based Education + Gamification Measurably Change Feeding Behavior?

**Short answer: Education improves knowledge strongly and quickly. Behavior change is real but requires the right design — knowledge alone is insufficient.**

### 3.1 The Indonesian Knowledge-Practice Gap: Why This Question Is Critical

**[STRONG]**

Research on MPASI practices in Indonesia documents a consistent and troubling pattern: **maternal knowledge does not automatically translate to feeding behavior change.**

Key findings from Indonesian MPASI studies (2021–2025):

- Mothers with higher formal education still initiate complementary feeding before 6 months when facing family pressure
- The PI-MPASI questionnaire (validated in Indonesia, Paediatrica Indonesiana 2023) measures 4 domains: timing, nutritional adequacy, safety, and responsiveness — and shows knowledge gaps in all four domains across education levels
- **Cultural override is the primary behavior driver:** when nenek or mertua are present and insist on traditional early foods, most mothers comply — even when they know it is incorrect
- **Perception barriers:** mothers perceive nutritious meal preparation as difficult and time-consuming; this perception drives shortcut behaviors regardless of knowledge

**The implication for Suppa:** Mode Edukasi is necessary but not sufficient. The app must also **reduce practical friction** (Mode Masak) and **provide ongoing reinforcement and accountability** (Mode Log Harian's weekly view) to bridge the knowledge-practice gap. Education + action scaffolding together — not education alone.

> **Sources:**  
>
> - PI-MPASI validation study. *Paediatrica Indonesiana*. 2023. [paediatricaindonesiana.org](https://paediatricaindonesiana.org/index.php/paediatrica-indonesiana/article/view/3110)  
> - Breastfeeding mothers' knowledge and MP-ASI provision. *ICISTech*. 2024. [icistech.org](https://icistech.org/index.php/icistech/article/download/237/209)

### 3.2 Mobile App Education for Child Nutrition: Evidence Review

**[EMERGING — promising but not yet definitive]**

A **systematic review (Goyal et al., 2022)** in *JMIR Pediatrics* assessed mobile apps for healthy behavior change and obesity prevention in children:

- Apps showed **significant improvements in dietary outcomes** particularly in multicomponent interventions (app + diary + feedback + education)
- **Single-function apps underperform** compared to apps combining logging, education, and behavioral feedback
- Evidence quality is often limited by: self-reported data, short follow-up periods (<6 months), small sample sizes

**Directly comparable RCTs:**

**MyKid'sNutrition (2023, Malaysia context):** An RCT protocol specifically designed to improve maternal nutritional knowledge and preschool children's nutritional status through a mobile app. Primary outcomes: maternal knowledge + child anthropometry. Results pending full publication but protocol design is methodologically sound.

> [PubMed 37596662](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37596662/)

**FLAGs App (Singapore):** RCT targeting infants 0–12 months with personalized guidance via web app. Found significant improvements in feeding practices compared to control group when personalization and push notifications were active.

> [PMC12496037](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12496037/)

**NutrienT Trial (France):** Smartphone app delivering complementary feeding guidelines to first-time parents — specifically targeting responsive feeding practices (not just food choice). Approach most closely mirrors Suppa's Mode Edukasi intent.

**[EMERGING] Key design factors that determine app effectiveness:**


| Design factor                       | Effect on outcomes                           | Suppa alignment                        |
| ----------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------- |
| Personalized feedback (not generic) | Strong positive                              | Yes — age-gated content, child profile |
| Behavioral monitoring (logging)     | Strong positive                              | Yes — Mode Log Harian                  |
| Goal setting                        | Moderate positive                            | Partially — streaks, weekly targets    |
| Push notification timing            | Moderate positive                            | Not yet specified                      |
| Social features                     | Mixed — depends on design                    | No community tab (OQ-3 decision)       |
| Gamification                        | Strong for engagement; moderate for behavior | Yes — streaks, badges, quizzes         |


> **Source:** Effectiveness of Mobile Apps in Promoting Healthy Behavior Changes. *JMIR mHealth*. 2022. [PMC9002598](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9002598/)

### 3.3 Gamification in Nutrition Education: The Evidence

**[MODERATE → STRONG for knowledge; MODERATE for behavior]**

**Systematic review and meta-analysis (Ródenas-Munar et al., 2021)** of gamification for diet and nutritional habits in children and adolescents:

- **Significant improvements in dietary behavior** in 67% of included studies
- Most effective elements: **immediate feedback, progress tracking, narrative/story elements, and level advancement**
- Less effective in isolation: leaderboards (competitive pressure can backfire), badges without meaning
- Meta-analytic effect: gamified interventions showed **moderate positive effect sizes** (d = 0.3–0.6) on dietary behavior

> **Source:** Ródenas-Munar M et al. "Gamification for the Improvement of Diet, Nutritional Habits, and Body Composition in Children and Adolescents." *Nutrients*. 2021. [PMC8308535](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8308535/)

**Gamification preferences in nutrition apps (Almeida et al., 2024):**

- Users preferred: **progress bars, streaks, and personalized challenges** over leaderboards
- Point systems increased daily engagement only when points connected to **visible, meaningful milestones** (not just accumulated numbers)
- **Quizzes specifically** showed high satisfaction and knowledge retention when: (a) short (3–5 questions), (b) immediately after content, (c) with explanation of correct answer, not just score

> **Source:** Almeida et al. "Gamification preferences in nutrition apps." *Frontiers in Nutrition*. 2024. [PMC11168059](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11168059/)

**Indonesian-specific evidence: Edusting app gamification for stunting education (2024):**

A study testing gamification methods in the **Edusting** app specifically for **Indonesian mothers' knowledge about stunting prevention**:

- Post-gamification scores significantly higher than pre-scores
- Mothers rated the gamified learning format as more motivating than leaflets or video lectures
- Short-form interactive modules (quiz-first format) showed highest completion rates

> **Source:** Implementation of Gamification in Edusting Application for Mothers' Knowledge of Stunting. ResearchGate. 2024. [link](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/386411424_The_Implementation_of_Gamification_Methods_in_the_Edusting_Application_as_an_Educational_Medium_to_Enhance_Mothers_Knowledge_of_Stunting)

**AI-Powered Gamified Chatbot for Parental Nutrition Knowledge (2025):**

A pilot study using a gamified chatbot with Online-Merge-Offline (OMO) strategy:

- Significant pre/post improvement in parental nutrition knowledge scores
- Participants rated gamified format as more engaging than traditional seminar-only format
- Hybrid (digital + in-person) outperformed digital-only — relevant for Posyandu integration potential

> **Source:** "A Pilot Study on AI-Powered Gamified Chatbot with OMO Strategy for Enhancing Parental Nutrition Knowledge." *MDPI Applied Sciences*. 2025. [mdpi.com](https://www.mdpi.com/2673-6470/5/2/13)

### 3.4 What the Research Says About the Right Gamification Design for Suppa

Synthesizing across all gamification studies, the following design principles are evidence-backed for Suppa's low-effort gamification (OQ-2 decision):


| Gamification element            | Evidence strength                              | Recommended Suppa implementation                                               |
| ------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| Daily logging streak            | Strong for habit formation                     | 7-day streak badge; streak shown on Today screen                               |
| Post-article quiz (3 questions) | Strong for knowledge retention                 | Quiz immediately after article; show answer explanation                        |
| Milestone badges                | Moderate — must be meaningful, not trivial     | "MPASI hari ke-7 dilewati!" badge, "5 warna Pelangi makan tercapai minggu ini" |
| Progress bar / completion ring  | Strong for daily motivation                    | Today's nutrition ring already serves this function                            |
| Points accumulation             | Moderate — only if connected to visible status | Show total points in profile; no redemption needed                             |
| Leaderboards                    | Weak / mixed — avoid for this audience         | Not recommended — can induce guilt in imperfect loggers                        |
| Personalized challenges         | Strong                                         | "Coba masukkan protein hewani 3x minggu ini" based on gap history              |

## Consolidated Assumptions and Risks

1. **[ASSUMPTION]** Riskesdas 2018 data still reflects current food diversity patterns. Indonesia's food environment is evolving; SEANUTS II (2019–2020) is newer but limited geographically to Java and Sumatra.
2. **[RISK]** The AOR 1.15 for dietary diversity on normal nutritional status is statistically significant but small. Dietary diversity is one factor among many (household wealth AOR 1.65 is stronger). Suppa should not overclaim causality between using the app and preventing stunting — the contribution is real but indirect.
3. **[ASSUMPTION]** Zinc gap hints will be actionable for Suppa users. This depends on zinc content data being available in the DKBM food database — which is not consistently complete for zinc across all foods. A simplified zinc-rich foods list may be more practical than per-meal zinc calculation.
4. **[RISK]** Knowledge-to-behavior gap is the primary threat to Mode Edukasi's impact claim. If Suppa measures only quiz scores (knowledge) and not feeding behavior change, it cannot validate the education-first positioning. A behavioral outcome proxy (logging frequency, recipe usage, food diversity score change over time) must be built into the analytics model from day one.
5. **[RISK]** Gamification fatigue is well-documented in long-term app use. Streaks and badges that feel motivating in week 1 can feel like obligations by week 8. Suppa needs a "grace mode" (miss one day without breaking the streak, or flexible streak definition) — the evidence supports compassionate streak design.
6. **[ASSUMPTION]** The Indonesian vitamin D figure (0.4% deficiency) is reliable. It almost certainly underestimates true deficiency due to measurement methodology in national surveys. Until better data is available, treat vitamin D as a content topic rather than a tracked metric.

## Recommendations

1. **Implement the WHO 8-food-group MDD framework as the scientific backbone of Pelangi makan.** The evidence is strong, the indicator is internationally validated, and it directly connects to the stunting outcome Indonesia cares most about. Use 5/8 groups as the weekly "well done" threshold.
2. **Track iron and zinc as primary micronutrient gap indicators in Mode Log Harian.** These are the two most prevalent deficiencies with the most actionable food-source remedies (animal-source foods). Surface food-specific hints (e.g., "Coba tambahkan hati ayam atau ikan teri minggu ini — kaya zat besi dan zinc") rather than numerical targets.
3. **Frame Mode Edukasi outcomes as behavior-adjacent, not behavior-direct.** The evidence shows apps change knowledge reliably; behavior change follows but is mediated by practical enablers. Mode Masak (reduce the effort of acting on knowledge) is Mode Edukasi's necessary companion. Design them as a linked pair: article → quiz → recipe that applies the lesson.
4. **Design gamification with compassion loops.** Evidence shows streaks and progress tracking drive engagement — but the streak must have a "grace day" design (miss one day, streak continues; miss two, streak resets but past progress is still celebrated). Shame-inducing design causes churn in this audience.
5. **Consider B2B2C Posyandu integration as a medium-term distribution play.** The government's stunting target (14.2% by 2029, per SSGI 2024) creates institutional appetite for digital tools that reach mothers in the MPASI window. Suppa's evidence base aligns with Perpres No. 72/2021 goals. A Posyandu partnership would provide validated distribution and increase tool credibility ("the Posyandu recommends Suppa").
6. **Include behavioral outcome proxies in the analytics model from day one.** Don't measure only quiz completion rates. Measure: food diversity score change over 4 weeks, frequency of iron/zinc-rich food appearance in logs, recipe diversity (how many unique recipes logged per week). These are the behavioral signals that validate the education-first positioning.
